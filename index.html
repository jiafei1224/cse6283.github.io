Course Description

This advanced course explores modern machine-learning algorithms for autonomous robots as embodied intelligent agents, examining how embodied AI differs from internet AI in perceiving raw sensory data, making decisions, and adapting to the physical world. It bridges classical techniques with recent advances in large-scale models, starting from the fundamentals of robotic manipulation, imitation learning, reinforcement learning, and policy optimization, and progressing to Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models and foundation models for robotics. Through debating cutting-edge papers and completing a hands-on project, students will learn to formulate problems, design algorithms, validate ideas experimentally, and engage with the research process.

Learning Outcomes

  • Develop a foundational understanding of robotic manipulation and the core principles of robot learning, including imitation learning and reinforcement learning.
  • Analyze and leverage foundation models for robotic manipulation tasks.
  • Apply and deploy core robot learning algorithms to tackle open-world manipulation problems.
  • Conduct independent research in robot learning and embodied AI.

Syllabus

This course offers a comprehensive journey through modern robot learning, beginning with the fundamentals of robotic manipulation grounded in Kevin Lynch and Frank Park's Modern Robotics and Russ Tedrake's Robotic Manipulation, covering rigid-body kinematics, contact mechanics, grasping, and motion planning. From this classical foundation, the course transitions into imitation learning, introducing behavior cloning, DAgger, and modern approaches such as Action Chunking Transformers (ACT) and Diffusion Policy. Students will then study reinforcement learning fundamentals, including Markov Decision Processes, value-based and policy-gradient methods, and policy optimization algorithms such as PPO and SAC, alongside practical considerations like offline RL and sim-to-real transfer. Building on these core robot learning algorithms, the course advances into the modern era of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models, examining landmark works including RT-1, RT-2, OpenVLA, MolmoAct, and π-0.5, with discussion of cross-embodiment learning, language-conditioned manipulation, and long-horizon reasoning frameworks such as SayCan, Manipulate-Anything, and Code-as-Policies. The final segment explores foundation models for robotics more broadly, drawing on recent publications in world models, generative simulators, and large-scale robot data efforts, preparing students to critically engage with the research frontier through paper discussions and a hands-on capstone project.

Ethical & Societal Implications

Throughout the course, we will integrate discussions of the ethical and societal implications of deploying learning-based robots in the physical world. As embodied AI systems move from controlled laboratory settings into homes, workplaces, and public spaces, they raise pressing concerns around safety, accountability, privacy, bias in training data, labor displacement, and the responsible collection and use of large-scale robot datasets. Topics such as imitation learning from human demonstrations, foundation models trained on web-scale data, and autonomous decision-making in open-world environments will be examined not only for their technical merits but also for the social risks they introduce, including failure modes in safety-critical settings, fairness across diverse users and environments, and the broader question of how much autonomy should be delegated to physical agents. Students will be encouraged to reflect on these dimensions in their paper discussions, capstone projects, and research framing, fostering a habit of considering ethical impact as an integral part of the engineering process rather than an afterthought.
